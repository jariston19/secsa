# Comprehensive Exam: Question Guidelines & Analytics
## Presentation outline (copy into PowerPoint / Google Slides)

**Audience:** Teachers, subject coordinators, program heads  
**Purpose:** Explain how to write quality MCQs using **Easy / Medium / Hard**, how **Bloom’s Taxonomy** strengthens that work behind the scenes, and how analytics turn results into action.

**Suggested deck length:** 18–22 slides (~25–35 minutes + Q&A)

## Slide 5 — Bloom’s Taxonomy (quick reference)

**On slide:**

| Level | Name | Starter verbs | Typical difficulty |
|-------|------|---------------|-------------------|
| L1 | **Remember** | define, list, recall | Easy |
| L2 | **Understand** | explain, summarize, classify | Easy–Medium |
| L3 | **Apply** | solve, compute, implement | Medium |
| L4 | **Analyze** | compare, differentiate, diagnose | Medium–Hard |
| L5 | **Evaluate** | justify, critique, select best | Hard |
| L6 | **Create** | design, propose, plan *(MCQ: “best design”)* | Hard |

**Speaker note:** L6 in MCQ usually means “choose the best design/solution,” not open-ended creation.

## Slide 6 — Difficulty criteria (define before writing)

**On slide — agree as a faculty, don’t tag by gut feel:**

| Difficulty | Target correct rate* | Expected time** | Bloom’s alignment |
|------------|---------------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Easy** | 70–90% | ~20 sec | L1–L2 |
| **Medium** | 40–70% | ~45 sec | L2–L4 |
| **Hard** | 20–40% | ~75 sec | L4–L6 |

\*After enough students take the exam — use for **calibration**, not guessing.  
\*\*With one-question-at-a-time exam UI.

**Speaker note:** If a “Hard” item is answered correctly by 90% of students, relabel it or rewrite it — otherwise difficulty analytics lie.

## Slide 7 — Writing Easy questions

**On slide:**
- **Bloom’s:** Remember, Understand (L1–L2)
- **Stem:** One clear fact or definition; no trick wording
- **Distractors:** Plausible but clearly wrong to someone who studied
- **Avoid:** Double negatives, “all of the above,” tiny typos as the only difference

**Example stem:**  
*“Which layer of the OSI model handles routing between networks?”*

**Checklist:** ☐ One concept ☐ One correct answer ☐ Topic tagged ☐ Bloom’s L1 or L2

---

## Slide 8 — Writing Medium questions

**On slide:**
- **Bloom’s:** Understand, Apply, light Analyze (L2–L4)
- **Stem:** Short scenario or requires one step of reasoning
- **Distractors:** Common mistakes from class (not joke answers)
- **Avoid:** Needing outside knowledge not covered in 1st year

**Example stem:**  
*“A student stores records in an array but inserts often at the front. Which structure is more appropriate?”*

**Checklist:** ☐ Requires thinking, not recall only ☐ Distractors = real misconceptions ☐ Bloom’s L2–L4

---

## Slide 9 — Writing Hard questions

**On slide:**
- **Bloom’s:** Analyze, Evaluate, Create-style (L4–L6)
- **Stem:** Multi-step reasoning or “best choice” under constraints
- **Distractors:** Each option must look reasonable to a partial learner
- **Avoid:** Obvious throwaway options — they lower the cognitive level

**Example stem:**  
*“Given this network diagram and symptom, which troubleshooting step should be performed **first**?”*

**Checklist:** ☐ Cannot be solved by elimination alone ☐ All distractors match stem complexity ☐ Bloom’s L4+

---

## Slide 10 — Distractor rules (most important slide)

**On slide:**
- Wrong answers must be **wrong for a teachable reason**
- Match **Bloom’s level of the stem** — L4 stem with L1 distractors = fake Hard question
- Document **why each distractor exists** (internal author notes)
- After the exam: most-chosen wrong option = **misconception to address**

**Visual:** One question with 4 options; annotate each distractor with the misconception it targets.

**Speaker note:** “Common wrong answers” in analytics only help if distractors were designed intentionally.

---

## Slide 11 — Question submission template

**On slide — every new item should include:**

1. Subject & topic  
2. Difficulty (Easy / Medium / Hard)  
3. Bloom’s level (L1–L6)  
4. Question stem  
5. Options A–D + correct key  
6. **Rationale:** why the correct answer is correct  
7. **Distractor notes:** what mistake each wrong option represents  
8. Optional: image / diagram reference  

**Handout idea:** One-page form teachers fill before entering the item in the system.

---

## Slide 12 — Exam composition (for set builders)

**On slide:**
- Balance **topics** across 1st-year units (no single topic dominating)
- Mix **Easy : Medium : Hard** per program guidelines (e.g. 30 : 50 : 20)
- Within each topic, span **L1–L4 minimum**; L5–L6 where appropriate
- **Do not** put all Hard items at the end — vary difficulty throughout

**Visual:** Sample 40-item table — columns: #, Topic, Difficulty, Bloom’s.

## Slide 14 — Cohort analytics (group / year level)

**On slide — metrics:**
- Average score & **pass rate**
- **Score distribution** (how many in 0–49, 50–59, 60–69, 70–79, 80–100)
- **Retention by topic** (% correct per unit)
- **Topic × difficulty heatmap** (% correct per cell)
- **At-risk list** (students below threshold, e.g. &lt; 60%)

| Pass vs fail | Donut or stacked bar |
| Topic retention | Horizontal bar chart |
| Topic × difficulty | **Heatmap** |
| Retake gain | Grouped bar (1st vs retake avg) |

## Slide 15 — Individual student analytics

**On slide — when you filter one student:**
- Overall score, pass/fail, attempt history
- **Score by topic** (which units stuck?)
- **Score by difficulty** (Easy OK but Hard weak → shallow retention)
- **Score by Bloom’s level** *(when tagged)* — surface vs deep retention
- Strengths / watch areas / weak areas (traffic-light summary)
- Per-attempt breakdown for retakes

**Recommended charts:**
| Metric | Chart type |
|--------|------------|
| Topic profile | **Radar chart** (student-friendly) |
| Difficulty breakdown | Bar chart |
| Bloom’s breakdown | Bar or stacked bar |
| Attempt over time | Line chart (score per attempt) |

**Speaker note:** A student strong on L1–L2 but weak on L4–L6 needs concept application work, not more flashcards.

## Slide 16 — Question-level analytics

**On slide — per item:**
- **Correct rate** (% answered correctly)
- **Difficulty alignment check** — labeled Hard but 85% correct? Recalibrate
- **Option distribution** — which wrong answer was picked most?
- Performance by **topic** and **difficulty** filters
- *(Advanced)* Discrimination index — top vs bottom scorers (needs ~30+ responses)

**Recommended charts:**
| Metric | Chart type |
|--------|------------|
| Hardest / easiest items | Sorted bar chart |
| Wrong answer patterns | Stacked bar (A/B/C/D counts) |
| Difficulty calibration | Scatter: labeled difficulty vs actual % correct |
| Item quality (later) | Scatter: discrimination index |

## Slide 17 — Analytics using difficulty

**On slide — questions to answer:**

1. **Class:** Did students retain **Medium** content or only **Easy**?
2. **Student:** High Easy + low Hard → broad but shallow memory
3. **Item:** Is our **Hard** bank actually hard?
4. **Time** *(if tracked)*: Hard correct in &lt; 10 sec → likely guess; Hard wrong after long time → genuine struggle

**Visual:** Heatmap mockup — rows = topics, columns = Easy / Medium / Hard, color = % correct.

**Action examples:**
- Whole cohort red on “Hard + Topic X” → schedule Topic X review week 1
- One student green Easy / red Hard → assign guided practice, not full re-teach

## Slide 18 — Analytics using Bloom’s Taxonomy

**On slide — questions to answer:**

1. **Remember vs Apply:** Can they recall terms but not use them?
2. **Analyze gap:** Topic scores OK at L1–L2 but collapse at L4+ → teaching depth issue
3. **Misalignment:** Bloom’s L5 item with L1 distractors → fix the question, not the students
4. **Curriculum:** Which cognitive levels did 1st year emphasize vs what 2nd year needs?

**Recommended charts:**
| Metric | Chart type |
|--------|------------|
| Cohort by Bloom’s level | Grouped bar (% correct L1–L6) |
| Student cognitive profile | Radar (6 axes = Bloom’s levels) |
| Bloom’s × topic | Heatmap |
| Label mismatch | Table: Bloom’s vs actual difficulty tier |

**Speaker note:** Bloom’s analytics require tagging at authoring time — start now even if dashboards come in a later release.

## Slide 19 — Reading patterns (cheat sheet for teachers)

**On slide:**

| Pattern | Likely meaning | Suggested response |
|---------|----------------|-------------------|
| Easy ✓, Hard ✗ | Shallow retention | Application labs, spaced review |
| One topic weak everywhere | Unit didn’t stick | Targeted module recap |
| Weak across all topics | Broad gap | Structured review program |
| L1–L2 ✓, L4+ ✗ | Memorized, not understood | Problem-based activities |
| Fast + wrong | Guessing | Exam skills + confidence |
| Slow + wrong | Concept gap | Office hours, worked examples |
| Most pick same wrong option | Shared misconception | Re-teach that specific idea |

## Slide 20 — Quality cycle (keep the bank healthy)

**On slide — after each exam cycle:**

1. **Review** heatmap & hardest items  
2. **Flag** mislabeled difficulty (too easy / too hard)  
3. **Rewrite** weak distractors  
4. **Retire** broken or leaked items  
5. **Fill gaps** in topic × difficulty × Bloom’s matrix  
6. **Document** changes for next year’s writers  

**Visual:** Circular PDCA diagram — Plan → Deploy exam → Analyze → Improve items.

## Slide 22 — Summary & next steps

**On slide:**
- **Students see:** Easy / Medium / Hard  
- **Teachers use:** Bloom’s Taxonomy to write and tag items  
- **Analytics answer:** Who needs help, on what topic, at what cognitive depth  
- **Next steps for faculty:**
  1. Adopt the question template (Slide 11)  
  2. Tag Bloom’s level on all new items  
  3. Run one calibration meeting after the first live exam  
  4. Use individual + cohort dashboards within two weeks of results  

**Closing line:** Good tags in → trustworthy analytics out → smarter remediation.

## Appendix C — Presenting Bloom’s without overwhelming the audience

**Framing script (30 seconds):**

> “The exam booklet and instructions will say Easy, Medium, and Hard — that’s what students prepare for. Behind the scenes, we use Bloom’s Taxonomy so every item tests the *right kind* of thinking. Easy items usually ask you to remember or understand; Hard items ask you to analyze or choose the best solution. When we tag both, the system can tell us not just that a student missed Network questions, but whether they forgot terms or can’t troubleshoot — and that changes how we help them.”
